# From Product Manager to Game Developer: Why I Made the Switch

*March 2026 · 6 min read*

## The Unexpected Path

Most game developers follow a straightforward path: study computer science, learn game engines, join a studio. My journey was... different.

I graduated from the University of International Relations in Beijing with a degree in Information Security and a minor in Economics & Finance. Then I spent three years as a Product Manager and Marketing Manager at Vackbot, a cybersecurity startup. Now I'm completing an MSc in Computer Game Engineering at Newcastle University.

Why would someone leave a successful product management career to start over in game development? The answer reveals something about what makes a great game designer.

## What Product Management Taught Me

At Vackbot, I wasn't just writing PRDs and managing sprints. I was:

- **Conducting user research** — interviewing clients, analyzing usage data, translating needs into features
- **Designing from zero** — building a brand identity, creating the product's visual language, establishing UX patterns
- **Coordinating cross-functional teams** — developers, designers, sales, and executives all had different priorities
- **Measuring outcomes** — 100+ channel trials in 6 months, 20+ client conversions, Red Herring Global Top 100

Every single one of these skills transfers directly to game design and production.

## The Game Design Connection

Game designers are, at their core, product managers for player experiences. Consider the parallels:

| Product Management | Game Design |
|-------------------|-------------|
| User research | Playtesting |
| Feature prioritization | Scope management |
| Sprint coordination | Production pipeline |
| Metrics & KPIs | Player analytics |
| Wireframing | Level blockouts |
| A/B testing | Difficulty tuning |

The tools are different, but the thinking is identical: understand your user, design for their needs, iterate based on data, ship on time.

## Why Games Specifically?

I've been a gamer my entire life, but that's not sufficient reason to change careers. What convinced me was realizing that games represent the most complex product design challenge that exists:

1. **Real-time systems** — Products serve web pages; games render 60+ frames per second
2. **Emotional design** — Products solve problems; games create feelings
3. **Multi-disciplinary** — Games require art, engineering, design, music, narrative, and business to work in harmony

The intersection of technical depth and creative expression is what drew me in.

## The MSc Experience

Newcastle's Computer Game Engineering program threw me into the deep end:

- **Rendering**: Building OpenGL pipelines, implementing PBR shaders, understanding GPU architecture
- **Physics**: Writing collision detection from scratch, constraint solvers, spatial partitioning
- **Networking**: Client-server architecture, state synchronization, latency compensation
- **Team Project**: Leading UI/UX for a 7-person multiplayer stealth game

The team project was where everything clicked. My product management experience made me naturally effective at:
- Breaking down the UI system into manageable modules
- Communicating with teammates about interface requirements
- Making design decisions with clear Problem → Decision → Outcome reasoning
- Shipping features on schedule while maintaining quality

## What I Bring to the Table

I'm not the strongest C++ programmer on my team. I'm not the best artist. But I might be the most effective at turning ideas into shippable features because I've done it professionally for three years.

The game industry needs people who can:
- Translate designer intent into technical specifications
- Coordinate between art, engineering, and design
- Make scope decisions that keep the project on track
- Communicate the "why" behind design choices

That's exactly what a product manager does. That's exactly what I do.
